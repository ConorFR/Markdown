1 Materials and methods

1.1 Experimental setup

Figure 1.1: Hucking estate map. Experimental mixtures plots were located as indicated by the blue rectangle. Three mature woodland sampling locations were located as indicated by the red circles.

All experiments and sample collection for this project were performed at the Hucking estate Woodland Trust site in Hollingbourne, Maidstone (Figure 1.1). An experimental plot was created at the Hucking estate with 3 treatment blocks each consisting of 27 plots of 36 trees in a 6 x 6 configuration with 2 m spacing between each tree, a larger plot consisting of 288 trees with 2 m spacing, and an area of no planting at the bottom right of each treatment block (Figure 1.2).

Figure 1.2: Hucking estate experimental setup. Triplicate treatment blocks consisted of randomised order plots of 36 trees, a mixed plot of 288 trees, and an area of no planting.

The tree species content in each plot can be seen in Table 1.1. The order of 36 tree plots was randomised across each treatment block to mitigate the influence of adjacent plots. For this experiment the plots of interest were: plot 7 which consisted of local provenance Quercus rober (Oak) only, plot 9 which consisted of 2080 provenance oak only, and the large mixed plot 28 which consisted of a mixture of the two oak provenances with local and 2080 provenances of Fraxinus excelsior (Ash), local and 2080 provenances of Prunus avium (Wild cherry), and 2050 and 2080 provenances of Castanea sativa (Sweet chestnut). Planting order can be see in Figure 1.3.

1.2 Tree Assessments

Height and DBH measurements were taken for all trees in single species and mixed plots. Lamas and primary shoot length were measured with a ruler for 10 randomly selected reachable shoots (1 - 2 m height) on 10 oak trees in the single species plots and 20 oak trees in the mixed species plot. The same trees and shoots used for shoot measurements were given a mildew score of either: 0 - no mildew, 1 - spots seen on some/all leaves, 2 - patches seen on some/all leaves, 3 - all leaves covered or lost. Missing trees were assumed to have died at a young age and for the purpose of analysis were considered the same as standing dead trees.

1.3 Soil chemistry

For soil chemistry analysis, 10 cm deep soil cores were taken after moving leaf littler aside. For the single species plots 7 and 9 in each treatment block two sets of six soil cores were taken, mixed together, reduced down to 1 kg, and were stored in sealable plastic bags and cooled with ice packs. Cores within a plot were collected within 1 metre of each other. In the mixed species plot 28, four sets of six cores were taken to give four samples in total. Two sets of six cores were also taken in the no planting zone for blocks 1 and 2 only. Sampling for soil chemistry was performed in March 2024. Samples were chilled overnight before dispatching to NRM, Coopers Bridge, Berkshire for soil chemistry analysis. The following analysis packages were performed: Analysis A082; textural classification, pH, available phosphorous, available potassium, available magnesium. Analysis 8421; carbon check report. Analysis 046; soil nitrogen.

1.4 Microbial communities

Between December 2023 and January 2024, five samples of soil were taken for single species plots, ten samples of soil in the mixed species plot, ten samples taken in the ‘no planting’ zone, and ten samples of soil taken in the mature woodland locations for each triplicate block of trees. Soil samples were taken with a soil corer, and photos of cores were taken after removing leaf litter. The top 0 - 5 cm of the core (Upper) was removed, transferred to a sterile sealable bag and was frozen. The remaining core (Lower) was also stored in a sterile sealable bag and was frozen. In the single species plots, soil samples were taken at four corners within the outer border of trees in the plot and another sample was taken in the centre. In the mixed species plots, soil samples were taken between adjacent Oak trees at 10 locations in the plot, ignoring the perimeter trees. In the ‘no planting’ plot, 10 samples were collected with an even distribution. In the mature woodland locations, soil samples were taken within 1 m of a mature Oak. Soil was removed from the freezer and sieved through a 2 mm sieve to remove stones before transferring to 50 mL falcon tubes until full. Samples were the frozen before dispatching on ice to Novogene UK Ltd., Cambridge science park, Milton road, Cambridge. Duplicate samples were prepared and sent for amplification either of the 18SV4 regions using primers: Forward - GCGGTAATTCCAGCTCCAA, Reverse - AATCCRAGAATTTCACCTCT, or of the ITS2 region using primers: Forward - GCATCGATGAAGAACGCAGC, Reverse - TCCTCCGCTTATTGATATGC.

1.4.1 Quality control

Primer and barcode removed read data was provided by Novogene in .fastq.gz format for each sample. Read quality was assessed using fastqc (Andrews, 2010), and the results compiled in a multiqc format for interpretation (Ewels et al., 2016).

1.5 Data analysis

For soil chemistry box plots, outliers were filtered from data using the interquartile range method and used for plotting boxplots. All points, including outliers, were overlaid. These plots were created using the ggplot2 (Wickham, 2016), and plotly (Sievert, 2020) packages of R studio 4.3.2 (R Core Team, 2023). Normality of the data was tested for each planting condition using the Shapiro-Wilk normality test for each chemistry characteristic. Homoscedasticity was tested using the Bartlett test. ANOVA tests were performed to compare chemistry characteristic values across the planting conditions if the requirements were met based on the results of the normality and homoscedasticity tests. Normality tests were performed for each planting condition separately within each chemistry characteristic and only taken forward for ANOVA if all planting conditions met the conditions for normality. Kruskall-wallis tests were used to compare chemistry characteristics across the planting conditions when normality or homoscedasticity were not found to be true. The Benjamini-Hochberg method was used to adjust p-values to correct for the multiple testing. Comparisons of tree survival, structure, and health were performed and plotted in the same way.

2 Results and discussion

2.1 Soil chemistry

Figure 2.1: Soil chemistry. Soil chemistry characteristics of samples taken from plots for local Oak or 2080 Oak (n = 6), a mixed species plot (n = 12), and pasture plot with no planting (n = 4). All samples were harvested in February 2024.

Soil chemistry analysis was performed for soil samples from areas of no planting, local provenance Oak plots, 2080 provenance Oak plots, mixed species plots which included both Oak provenances alongside local and 2080 provenance Ash trees, local and 2080 provenance wild cherry, and 2050 and 2080 provenance sweet chestnut trees. Three biological replicates existed for all plot types. Two technical replicates were prepared from two of the biological replicate plots for the areas of no planting (n = 4), two technical replicates were prepared for all three biological replicates of the single species plots plot (n = 6), four technical replicates were prepared from the mixed species plots to reflect the larger plot sizes (n = 12). Soil chemistry characteristics were compared across the four planting conditions (Figure 2.1). Soil inorganic carbon was found to be < 0.1 % for all samples. Soil textural classifications for individual samples can be found in Supplementary Table 4.1. When comparing characteristics using ANOVA or Kruskall-Wallis test, based on normality and homoscedasticity assessment, no significant differences were observed before or after Benjamini-Hochberg multiple test correction across the four planting conditions for any of the characteristics investigated here (Supplementary Table 4.2).

2.2 Tree comparisons

2.2.1 Tree survival

Figure 2.2: Oak tree survival. The death rates (A), and dieback rates (B) of oak trees from local and 2080 provenances in single species plots or plots containing a mixture of species and provenances were calculated for the three replicate setups at the Hucking estate mixtures experiment.

Trees that had died and were previously removed, or that were dead at the time of measurement were noted and the rate of death was compared between planting conditions (Figure 2.2A). The results of the Shapiro-Wilk test found that death rate data for local oak (p = 0.22) mixture local oak (p = 0.78), and mixture 2080 oak (p = 1) were normally distributed. The death rate data for 2080 oak planted in single species plots was found not to be normally distributed (p = 0). The Levene test for homoscedasticity did indeed find equal variances across the planting conditions (p = 0.543), however due to the small sample sizes a non-parametric testing method was used to compare death rates across planting conditions. A significant difference in death rates were observed across the oak trees under different planting conditions (Kruskal-Wallis test: H(3) = 8.37, p = 0.039). Pairwise comparisons using the Dunn’s test revealed that the death rate was significantly lower for 2080 provenance oak trees in single species plots than local oak trees in single species plots (p = 0.00813) and mixed plots (p = 0.0171). Mixed plot 2080 oak trees had a death rate that was only found to be significantly lower than the local oak planted in single species plots (p = 0.0295), but was not significantly lower than the local oak death rate when planted in the mixed species plot (p = 0.0546). This could suggest an improvement in the survival of local oak trees when planted in a mixed species plot. Indeed it has been suggested previously that mixed species stands may experience increased resistance to hazards such as windthrow and pests (Griess and Knoke, 2011). Improved survival rates for other tree species have also been reported when grown in mixed versus monoculture stands (Griess et al., 2012).However, reduced survival rates of oak trees resulting from woody vegetation competition have also been observed (Jensen and Löf, 2017).

Rates of observed dieback across oak trees from local and 2080 provenances and planted in single species or mixed species plots were compared (Figure 2.2B). For all oak conditions, the data was found to be normally distributed (Local: p = 0.302; 2080: p = 0.177; Mixture local: p = 0.358; Mixture 2080: p = 0.968), and homoscedasticity across planting conditions was also observed (p = 0.58). No significant difference in dieback rates between the planting conditions were observed (ANOVA: F(3) = 0.0407, p = 0.988).

Death and dieback rates were compared for all oak trees across the replicate blocks to confirm that no significant differences in death rates (Kruskal-Wallis test: H(2) = 0.746, p = 0.689) or dieback rates (ANOVA: F(2) = 1.26, p = 0.329) were observed across the blocks (Supplementary Figure 4.1).

2.2.2 Tree structure

Figure 2.3: Oak tree structure. The height (A) and DBH (B) were measured for all oak trees of local or 2080 provenance planted in either single species plots or mixed species plots (n = 36).

For the remaining live trees, height and DBH measurements were compared for oak trees from local or 2080 provenances planted in single species plots or in mixed species plots (Figure 2.3). No significant differences were observed for height (Kruskall-Wallis: H(3) = 5.2, p = 0.157) or DBH (ANOVA: F(3) = 2.6, p = 0.0523) between planting conditions.

2.2.3 Tree health

Figure 2.4: Oak tree shoot growth. The primary (A) and lamas (B) shoot growth was measured in centimetres for 10 shoots of 10 trees in the 3 biological replicate plot setups (n = 300), or for 20 trees in mixed species plots (n = 600).

Statistical comparison of primary shoot growth across the oak tree planting conditions revealed significant differences between all conditions when tested both parametrically and non-parametrically, and with or without filtering outliers using the interquartile range method (Tukey, 2008). Visual inspection of the primary shoot growth data in Figure 2.4A demonstrates that this is likely caused by the large sample sizes of non-normal distribution (Shapiro Wilk. Local: p = 0.0051, 2080: p = 0.00000401, Mixture: p = 0.000000124). The same was observed when statistically testing the differences in lamas shoot growth, and again visual inspection of the lamas shoot data suggests that the high significance is likely an underlying effect of large non-normally distributed data (Figure 2.4B).

Figure 2.5: Oak tree mildew load. In each of the three biological replicate plot setups, mildew scores were assigned by visual inspection of leaves on a single shoot with 10 shoots investigated for each tree and 10 trees in each single species plot (n = 300) or 20 trees in the mixed species plots (n = 600). Mildew categories: 0 - no mildew; 1 - spots seen on some/all leaves; 2 - large patches seen on some/all leaves, 3 - all leaves completely covered.

The frequencies at which shoots were assigned to the different levels of mildew infection were compared across the planting conditions (Figure 2.5). Statistical comparison by a chi-squared test revealed no significant association between oak planting conditions and mildew scores (X2 = 11.8, p = 0.0674).

2.3 Soil microbial communities

2.3.1 Quality control

FastQC was used to assess the quality of raw read data after primer and barcode removal by Novogene (Andrews, 2010). FastQC results for individual raw read files were assembled into consolidated reports for 18SV4 and ITS2 amplification separately using multiQC (Ewels et al., 2016).

18SV4:


All 464 samples passed the sequence quality checks of fastQC in addition to the ‘per base N content’ check, indicating that no issues occurred with the sequencing process itself. However, all samples contained high proportions of duplicate reads and clear over-representation of a small number of short reads. This suggested that amplification of short, conserved regions shared across taxa occurred (Lindahl et al., 2013). Indeed, investigation of these short fragments through a nucleotide BLAST returned matches to the 18S rDNA regions for multiple fungal taxa (Sayers et al., 2022). Duplication of sequences might also be the result of bias with the 18SV4 primers (Zimmermann et al., 2024), or the formation of chimeras (Lindahl et al., 2013). The over representation of sequences is likely the cause of the skewed distributions of GC contents, sequence lengths, and per base sequence content, which all samples failed. All samples passed the adapter content check, as reads have been primer and barcode removed by Novogene.

ITS2:


All 476 samples passed the sequence quality checks and the ‘per base N content’ check, indicating no issues with sequencing itself. Similarly to the 18SV4 sequencing data, the ITS raw reads present large proportions of duplication and over representation of a small number of short fragments. Again, this is likely due to the amplification of short, conserved regions shared across taxa which can also occur for ITS2 amplification (Ihrmark et al., 2012; Lindahl et al., 2013). This issue can be exacerbated in soil samples where DNA degradation is common (Levy-Booth et al., 2007; Taberlet et al., 2012). As a result of the over representation, distributions of GC content, sequence lengths, and per base sequence content are skewed to failure for all samples. Despite some apparent issues with the raw read data, the processing pipeline PIPITS has multiple quality control steps built-in, including trimming low-quality bases and removing short reads, dereplication to remove duplicate reads and therefore reduce redundancy, and also flagging and removing chimeras that may arise during PCR (Gweon et al., 2015). Unlike the 18SV4 amplicon read data, the ITS2 amplicon read data has 8 samples which failed the adapter content check suggesting that some adapter sequences have persisted. However, PIPITS leverages CutAdapt during pre-processing to remove any persistent adapter sequences (Gweon et al., 2015; Martin, 2011).
